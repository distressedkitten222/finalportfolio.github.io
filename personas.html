Personas and Storyboarding for a Public Vending Machine

Overview

Objective: To explore the UIUX research process, by creating user personas and storyboarding a user experience for a public interface.

Role: UX Researcher, Storyboarder

Public Interface: Vending Machine

Vending machines provide quick and easy access to a variety of snacks and drinks; a vending machine interface makes the process of buying a snack quicker and more convenient by eliminating interaction between the customer and salespeople. Users can view which items are in stock, choose from multiple payment options, input the code of the desired item, and collect it from the slot it falls into.

Key Components

  • Display window: enables users to view items in stock, prices, and item codes. Users can also watch their item fall and notice if it gets stuck.
  • Keypad with buttons: enables users to input the code of the item they want.
  • Bill slot: users can insert bills for payment.
  • Coin slot: users can insert coins for payment.
  • Change collection slot: change is left here for users to retrieve it.
  • Card reader: users can swipe a debit, credit, or student card here for payment.
  • LCD panel: displays messages throughout the process to keep users informed.
  • Button to cancel transaction: enables user to stop the transaction before receiving an item and initiates the refund.
  • Door flap: users can push this in to access the area where the item falls.
  • Graphics and text: these give the user instructions on how to use the machine.

Research Process

User Observation

I observed several users of an on campus vending machine, during the day and later at night, to find general patterns of behaviour, outliers, and pain points. I made the following key observations:

  • All users used the card reader for payment (credit/debit cards or student ID cards).
  • All users swiped their card through the card reader multiple times, either flipping the card around or changing the direction in which they swiped.
  • Most users looked at the screen after swiping their cards.
  • More users approached the vending machine at night than in the day.
  • Most users purchased one item. One user purchased multiple items, repeating the swipe-select-collect process multiple times - they apologized repeatedly to the person next to them for the time it took.

User Interviews

I conducted three interviews with students who had used campus vending machines previously. My main questions were:

  1. What motivates you to use a campus vending machine?
  2. Is there a typical pattern to the times that you use vending machines?
  3. Which method of payment do you like best?
  4. Have you experienced any challenges in the process of using campus vending machines?
  5. What would be your ideal way of conducting a full transaction at a vending machine?

Through these interviews, I was able to gain deeper insight into their user experience and their pain points. Important findings include:

  1. There are distinct motivations to use campus vending machines: first, to stock up when the user has a lot of points to spend on their student card; second, as a last resort when no other store is accessible.
  2. Users tend to use vending machines more often at night, during weekdays, and during colder months of the year.
  3. Users prefer card payments (debit, credit, or student card) since they do not typically carry cash or coins; however, it is unclear which way to swipe the card and whether it's worked.
  4. Other challenges: using a credit card loads a default $4 into the machine even if a user only wants one item; there's no indication of how much time a user has to make selections before the machine shuts down the transaction; and it is annoying that the collection door is so low down.
  5. Some users would like to swipe a card at the machine and have it just take the cost of the desired item, so there is no waste of money; some would like to enter a total amount of money, add items until meeting that total, and be able to buy them all at once instead of one by one, saving time.

User Personas

I created empathy maps to represent two user personas.

Bulk Buyer Betty

Betty is a college student who needs to stock up on snacks, using up the points left on her student card so she doesn’t have to spend her own money.

But she can't tell which way to swipe her card; she has to repeat the entire process for each item she wants to buy; and the collection slot is low, which is annoying because she has to bend down so many times to collect her items.

Betty represents users who want to buy a lot of items from a nearby vending machine, using points instead of going to a store, but wish it took less time.

Last Resort Liam

Liam is a student who stays up late studying and needs a sugar hit; since stores are closed, he reluctantly uses a vending machine despite the higher prices.

But he can't tell which way to swipe his card to make it work; since he's paying with card, the machine takes a default $4 from him, even though he only wants one thing worth $1.75; and he can't tell how much time he has to make choices, so he has to rush.

Liam represents users who use vending machines when there is no other option, and value being able to buy just what they need and move on.

Storyboard

Persona: Liam

Then I created a storyboard for Liam.

Liam is studying late in the library and starts to feel sleepy. He needs to finish his assignment tonight, so he needs some sugar or caffeine.

Liam sees that it's too late to go to a convenience store. He also doesn't want to go far from the library.

Liam goes to the vending machine in the library to look for something there.

Liam looks at the options through the display window and finds Gatorade, with item code E1.

Liam swipes his credit card through the card reader, loading money into the vending machine. It takes $4 by default.

Liam uses the buttons on the keypad to input his selection, E1. The screen shows his current cash balance and updates with his selection as he pushes the buttons.

Liam sees that he still has a $2.25 balance, but doesn't know if he has time to calculate out to use it all up. He quickly uses the keypad to select chocolate pretzels (item code D2), which he knows he has enough money for.

Liam checks the screen to see his new balance. It isn't enough to get anything else, so he doesn't input any more codes.

Done making choices, Liam pushes the collection door flap in so he can remove his Gatorade and chocolate pretzels, which were dropped into the collection area.

Having successfully got his items, Liam takes a drink of Gatorade to renew his energy.

Liam goes back to work with his snacks, having found enough sugar to be more alert.

Learning

I learned a lot from this project, but two things surprised me:

  • There are correct and incorrect ways to interview; small differences in the way interview questions are framed can result in quite different interview 'findings'.
  • It is possible to identify different personas from just one user interview - this also shows the importance of interviews instead of just observation.

Next Steps

I could move forward by attempting to design a vending machine interface that addresses these issues! The challenge lies in addressing multiple personas' issues together.
